Research in the group is focused on human genetic variation and understanding its role in human disease using high-throughput sequencing technologies. Projects involve development of computational and statistical methods, analysis of data from DNA sequencing technologies and disease association analysis. Two major themes of research in the lab are:

1. Methods for accurate and comprehensive re-sequencing of human genomes. Although human genome sequencing has advanced rapidly, DNA sequencing using short Illumina reads does not provide information about haplotypes and is unable to identify variants in repetitive regions of the genome. We have developed a number of computational tools (e.g. HapCUT2) for sequence-based haplotyping that work with diverse sequencing technologies. We are developing methods for the detection of variants in duplicated genes using whole-genome Illumina sequencing as well as single-molecule long read sequencing technologies. The goal is to enable the detection of disease-associated variants in these regions in both rare and complex human diseases.

2. Human genetics using large-scale DNA and RNA-seq datasets. We have recently identified disease-causing variants in individuals with early-onset diabetes using large-scale targeted sequencing. Using monogenic diabetes as a model, we are investigating the genetic overlap between rare and complex diseases with the goal of identifying reduced penetrance disease mutations.
Predicting the functional impact of DNA sequence variants is a fundamental problem in human genomics. A subset functional mutations act by disrupting/modifying pre-mRNA splicing for a single gene or for multiple genes. We are interested in leveraging large-scale RNA-seq datasets to understand the genetics of local and genome-wide aberrant splicing.
